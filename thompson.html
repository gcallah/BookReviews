Review of America's Revolutionary Mind

C. Bradley Thompson. 2019. America's Revolutionary Mind
Encounter Books: New York.
$32.99
447 pages

The book under consideration here is intended as the first half of a study on the American founding: this volume focuses on the Declaration of Independence and the causes of the American Revolution, while the second volume is intended to focus on the United States Constitution. Thompson feels that the bi-partite division of these works, as well as their complementarity, reflects a fundamental reality about the United States: as he puts it, "The relationship between the current and the proposed volume is virtually identical to the connection that Abraham Lincoln saw between the Declaration of Independence and the Constitution" (xi).

Thompson is certainly not one to hide his light under a bushel. In the Preface he tells us, "I have attempted to write a book that is original, comprehensive, and transformative" (xiii). Now, in my understanding of hwat an hitorical work should be aiming at, it would be to determine what actually happened in the past, and how what happened at some particular time followed from what happened at earlier times. Such a work may, in fact, wind up being "original, comprehensive, and transformative," but to set out with those goals in mind is, I suggest, a deformation of the character of historical research.

To elaborate upon this point, consider Thompson's distress that the works of Bernard Bailyn and Gordon S. Wood from the late sizties remain the most influential books on understanding the Revolution: "Surely, though, it should not remain the case that the most influential books on the American Revolution were written in the 1960s" (xii). Ok, why not? Mathematicians never exhibit anxiety over the fact that the most influential understanding of the relationship of the sides of a right triangle was arrived at several thousand years ago. No, the Pythagorean Theorem is correct, and mathematicians accept that this is right (for Euclidean spaces) and build on that result. Perhaps it just happens to be the case that Bailyn and Wood "got things right" in the 1960s: well, so what? And if they did not get things right, then certainly how long ago they made their errors is irrelevant. In fact, Thompson's concern about how "old" these works are seems to fly in the face of his repeated assertions that the American founders were espousing "timeless" truths. If their truths from the 1700s are not to be dismissed as being "old," then why shoud the works of Bailyn and Woods suffer this fate?

Thompson suffers from a common tendency to create a false dichotomy: either someone ignores historical circumstances and accepts "timeless" values, or they are an unprincipled "relativist," thinking that there are no standards of right and wrong, only whatever some particular society happens to regard as right or wrong. That this dichotomy is false can be illustrated with a simple analogy: let's say I want to travel from Hawaii to Easter Island by the fastest route possible?

Well, if I lived in Hawaii in 1200 A.D., the fastes route would have been by catamaran. But today, a jet plane can surely get me there faster. Does that mean that "the truth" about the fastest route is relative? Of course not: catamaran really was the fastest route in 1200, and jet plane really is the fastest route today. In 1200, someone who suggested "walking" as the fastest route from Hawaii to Easter Island would have been objectively wrong, just as today someone recommending "catamaran" would be objectively wrong. And in the future, when we have constructed hyper-space tunnels, the answer "jet plane" will be objectively wrong.

In fact, it is only by considering historical circumstances that we can really, objectively determine what would have been the best action available to an actor in the circumstances in which they found themselves. If we want to criticize the Polynesians for traveling for Easter Island by catamaran, the only coherent way to do so is by pointing out that, in their actual, historical circumstances, they in fact had a better alternative. It is ridiculous to argue, "These Polynesian explorers were fools: why didn't they just take a jet plane?" when there were no jet planes available to them.

Thompson goes on to contend that he has developed "a new approach to history writing that I shall call new moral history" (6). This approach, he says, "emphasizes thinking, judging, choosing, and acting individuals over large-scale social processes moved by unforeseen forces" (6). But to anyone who has studied the historiographical work of, say, Dilthey, Collingwood, Oakeshott, or Mises, this approach will hardly seem new.
