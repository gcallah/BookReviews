Review of Cities Alive

Cities Alive: Jane Jacobs, Christopher Alexander, and the Roots of the New Urban Renaissance Michael W. Mehaffy, Sustasis Press, Portland, 2017

"The city itself would become a kind of machine for serving up whatever we needed or wanted" (p. 20).

"Formal goverment, in [Elinor Ostrom's] view, needs to be supplanted with many other forms of governance, formal and informal, across many scales" (22). (The recent work of Nassim Taleb has emphasized the importance of scale to sound governance as well.)

"Most people are now at least generally familiar with 'systems theory,' and with fractals, algorithms, strange attractors, network science, and the like" (35). This is a strange passage. For instance, what are "algorithms" doing in there? "People" have been familiar with algorithms for over two thousand years: we still teach "Euclid's algorithm" and the "sieve of Eratosthenes" to computer science students today. One of the founders of the "old sciences," Isaac Newton himself, devised an algorithm for finding numeric roots. The existence of these and many other algorithms hardly stopped the terrible surgery done on cities in the mid-20th century... so why are they relevant in this list? Even some of the other items, such as fractals and strange attractors, while only recognized more recently, still appear more like trendy terms thrown in to make the practical knowledge of how to build livable cities more "scientific." People around the world, using traditional wisdom, built aesthetic and life-encouraging cities for centuries before they had ever heard of "fractals" or "strange attractors": one can help but wondering if Mehaffy is still in thrall to scientism, albeit a more hip version than "old-fashioned" Newtonian science.

Now, it is true that the increasing recognition, in scientific circles, of what Jane Jacobs called "complex orders" (in contrast to simple orders, dealt with by classical mechanics, and randomness, dealt with by statistical mechanics), can represent a step forward. But if it does, it is through warning scientists, and, more importantly, adherents of scientism, that these complex orders are not susceptible to theoretical mastery, but instead can only be (partially) tamed through experiential knowledge.
